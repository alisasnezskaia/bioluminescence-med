bioluminescence
— med

The Mediterranean glows at night and almost nobody is tracking it at scale. This project builds a bioluminescence probability field from satellite proxies — SST anomalies, chlorophyll-a, upwelling signals — trained on sparse ship observations, then flags the bloom events that don't fit the seasonal pattern.

PyTorch xarray cartopy CMEMS ERA5 XGBoost LSTM autoencoder spatial block CV SHAP
the question

Can you predict where the ocean is likely glowing tonight using nothing but satellite observables — and catch the events that don't fit the pattern?

Ground truth: MBARI ship logs + Copernicus biogeochemistry reanalysis

Building the proxy set
SST anomaly vs climatology, chlorophyll-a, mixed layer depth, PAR × CHL interaction, wind stress curl for upwelling detection. The notebook that shows you actually read the papers.
domain features
03 · Model
Probability field
XGBoost regression against MBARI labels. Spatial block CV — not random splits, because random splits leak autocorrelation. One decision that separates people who've thought about this from people who haven't.
XGBoost · spatial CV
04 · Anomalies
What doesn't fit
LSTM autoencoder on the probability time series per grid cell. High reconstruction error means something unusual is happening. Outputs a dated catalogue of anomalous bloom events with severity scores.
LSTM · anomaly detection
05 · Atlas
Making it look like a paper
Seasonal probability maps, bloom event overlays, SHAP waterfall charts. Honestly took as long as the model. Cartopy coastline rendering at the right resolution is genuinely fiddly.
cartopy · SHAP · matplotlib
06 · Validation
Checking against the literature
Two published papers on Mediterranean bioluminescence — one from the Ligurian Sea, one from the Adriatic. Does our bloom catalogue overlap with theirs? Where it doesn't: why not?

what i learned

honest notes

01
Spatial CV is non-negotiable. My first run used random splits and got suspiciously good scores. Switched to block CV — held out 4° × 4° spatial chunks — and the numbers dropped ~15%. That's the real number. The first one was memorising autocorrelation.
02
PAR × CHL interaction beat SST anomaly as the top feature. SHAP confirmed it, and it makes physical sense — bioluminescence in dinoflagellates is partly a stress response to photosynthetic conditions. I didn't expect it to rank first but it did, every fold.
03
The autoencoder flagged the 2018 Adriatic bloom before I knew it existed. Found a 2021 paper afterwards that confirmed it. That was the moment this stopped feeling like a homework exercise.
04
Cloud cover is a real problem. Some cells near the Ligurian coast are missing 40+ days a year in winter. Rather than pretending gap-filled values are real observations, I flagged low-confidence predictions separately. The model knows what it doesn't know.
05
matplotlib's perceptually uniform colormaps don't work well for probability fields. Viridis and cividis wash out the spatial structure. Ended up building a custom diverging map — dark ocean blue to bioluminescence teal — which made the patterns readable at a glance.

still open
→
Replace XGBoost with a ConvLSTM to forecast spatial fields rather than single-cell series. The spatial structure is being thrown away right now — that's the obvious next step.
→
Calibrate the probability outputs. They're not well-calibrated against sparse MBARI labels. Isotonic regression or conformal prediction would fix this and matter a lot for any operational use.
→
Extend to the Black Sea. CMEMS coverage is good there and it has its own distinct ecology. I suspect the model won't generalise without retraining but it would be interesting to see exactly where it breaks.
